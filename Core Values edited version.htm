AM I WHO I THINK I AM??

 

 

Core Values

After having gone on my first adventure abroad, little has motivated in my adult life as much as travelling. Forgo the mundane acts like that of a ticket getting scanned or sitting on a plane for hours, spending time in airports drudging through visa processing and luggage recovery. That is not what thrills me. Neither is it the hotels and room service, exotic theme parks, bars, shops, historical buildings nor landmarks. What truly inspires me is the interaction among people. Being a foreigner among strangers is the perfect setting for a display of my listed core values. I have always witnessed repeatedly when I interact with strangers in a new place. Adventure, humility, altruism, self-discipline and trust/reliability represent the best traits in humanity to me.

A good example of this is a local host. These people have a desire to be trusted and in turn to trust in you. A fair and equal social exchange becomes the main currency among people.

The temporary host, that is basically a stranger to you, is happy to go on the small adventure of showing you around. They are eager to interact with a foreigner and break away from the tediousness of everyday life. They come alive not just through the virtue of seeing life from an outsiders point of view but by sharing the best around to offer. A local host will display their humility when showing you around their environment. When asking about the place you originate from they too want a display in humility. No one wants to hear a put down such as, well yeah, where I come from is much better than here it is safer, cleaner and warmer with McDonalds available on every corner...etc. The person of the native area will altruistically go out of their way to ensure that your accommodation is met whether by taking you to shelter, making sure there is food to eat or even to just help you reach a place to have those things met. These core values from foreign hosts have shaped the way I try to behave as a human. I do this for everyone irrespective if I know them well or not at all.

Ethical Decision Making

Fortunately, no major ethical dilemmas have crossed my path in adulthood. However, there is one that I made as a teenager. It had strong effects on both me, and my direct family and will certainly be informative, if not insightful, to those that read or assess this content.

Mine and my brothers parents went through a divorce, such as so many others have had to go through. On the surface it appeared civil enough but psychologically it was damaging to a large degree. I feel it had a deep impact on my father, especially.

At the time decisions were made hastily as emotions ran high. My father moved out of the family home/lifestyle block (small farm) to the spare bedroom of his parents house. I went to see him after school one day. He was in a state that words to describe it would be disrespectful so Ill leave it to your own imagination. It was not how I wanted to view one of my parentals. After witnessing my fathers circumstances, I returned to my family home. I took stock of the state of my mother. Then I proceeded to instruct my mother to pack her things and leave so that my father could return. As a teenager, it shocked and surprised me that my mother did as I had asked. I told her what I said on the belief that she would suffer less than my father for being forced out. Being put in a situation to feel I needed to parent my mother and father did not lead to rainbows, fairy tales and happy endings. The consequences of family trauma impact the family to this day. For better or for worse, I made it. Despite having years of reflection and experience, I cant think of any reason to betray my younger self and change my actions.  I think of the teenage boy that I was, who had two choices: speak up or say nothing. In the aggregate, I did what my mind compelled me to do, which was to try for the best in a disastrous situation.

 

How my culture has influenced my values and identity

There isnt anything that doesnt affect ones idea of themselves. It is the process of navigating the landscape of existence that pushes and pulls on you leading you to make decisions on what works better or worse for you as you move forward toward the end. The different cultures and societies I lived in have shaped my values and identity as much as my homeland.

The examples of the more kiwi side of my personality include:

·        Playing sport is of great value, especially team sport.

·        Being on time is good, being early is better

·        Owning a home on a piece of land and raising children. This is the kiwi dream and likely the most rewarding thing I could ever do with my life, so long as it is managed properly

·        Time spent on the beach or in the outdoors is a must. This is expected at least once a week as it is imperative to both physical and mental health

·        Camping, fishing, hunting, tramping (all part of a childs upbringing)

·        the same level of respect and attention to all people regardless of social status. I should be just as relaxed speaking with someone who is either struggling economically or affluently successful. I should not be a braggart, as well. No tall poppy syndrome as that is a stain on our reputation.

examples of trying to work with others but running into resistance or tension

In the United Kingdom I was a part of a selected crew to open a new location of a restaurant bar. The original location set the team up without authorizing anyone the position of front of house manager or team leader.

Each of us had slightly different ideas on how running the till system and service should work. Each implemented their own style while working. I noticed it was inefficient and was causing inconsistent service times. Having a good grasp of the protocol that needed to be implemented, I took charge and set a single system in place. This wasnt easily accepted by one of the male team members. I took the time and effort to state clearly why a single system was preferable. It provided better service to the customers and communication with the kitchen. The person could not be bothered work in a cohesive group and to see the results, so eventually returned back to the original location. I continued to run the location for the duration of my stay in London. I developed and managed my own team and continued on until the location was forced out by the university. The main reason being it was the only business in the area not run by the University and therefore taking patronage and money away from its other locations.  That speaks for itself on how stepping up and creating a cohesive work place fosters success.
